Overview


This project was the result of a 48-hour Hackathon, primarily collaborating with software developers. The main goal was to design and build a reponsive website that pulls data out of a database and allows users to insert data into the database.

Problem Space



Grace is a driver in Toronto who feels fed up with expensive parking fees around the city. She’s an independent individual who occasionally finds sweet free parking spots around the city and wishes she had a way to remember the locations. She already shared a couple of her spots with her friends, but why don’t we just make this process a lot easier for her by developing a platform that meets her needs?

An empathy map was created to frame the problem.

Pain Points

1) Locating and remembering parking spots is very challenging.

2) There is no dedicated location to share her thoughts with others.

Solution

Introducing Spotter, a web based platform and sharing community which allows users to find, share and leave parking spot reviews.

Next Steps



The following features were explored but not implemented due to scope limitation:

1. Notify users to let them know when to move their car
2. Track which spots are available in real time
3. Help users find the best rate
4. Airbnb, but for parking spots (Option for users to rent out their parking spot and generate revenue)




Reflections and Learning



1) Importance of knowing your constraints

As a designer, I had many extra features that I wanted to add, however, after talking to the developers, I learned that due to time and technical constraints not every idea would be feasible. Going forward, it will be important to be cognizant of these constraints when designing, to avoid scope creep.



2) Consistent & Effective Communication

I learned that designers and developers think very differently and so regular check-ins were very important. For example, as a designer, I added a function to add pictures in a review. However, my developers could not understand the purpose of the function as from their point of view, it did not make sense and seemed useless. Ultimately, I had to explain in a stand-up that the app was not about what they wanted, but about what our users wanted. In addition, our competitive and comparative research showed that adding pictures lent a lot of credence to reviews. Thus, regular and effective communication is key when you are working in a team dynamic, especially if it's cross-functionally.
