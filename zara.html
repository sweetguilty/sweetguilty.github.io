#Overview




In 2020, Inditex, the largest apparel company that owns Zara, announced it would invest 3 billion dollars on boosting the e-commerce sector. While Zara is a very popular retail apparel company, it holds a reputation among many users (such as in the tweet above) for having an unpleasant user experience on their website. This case study will explore the pain points and propose a solution to address these user frustrations.

#Problem


Zara has an incredible selection of clothes where anyone can find something that would match their taste and style. The website is minimalistic and elevated which matches their brand image, however the shopping experience is not as clean and simple as their visuals.

Not being able to easily navigate and browse through their large section of products means Zara would be losing potential users and customers.



#Needs


1) Users
Users need to seamlessly navigate through the site so they can discover and easily browse for items.

2) Business
Business’ needs to keep the site high-end and elevated.



#Painpoints


#HMW

How might we make features discovery easier?
How might we make the navigation process learnable and consistent?
How might we keep the high end elevated elements intact?

#FinalResults

This case study proposed small, but impactful changes to provide a much better experience for users while keeping the brand image intact. Our team focused on redesigning the three pain points. Now with these proposed updates, users will have a straight forward shopping experience without making any large sweeping changes. This transformation will generate more revenue for Zara and turn browsers into buyers.

#Discovery


User interviews and contextual inquiries were conducted to gather research. What was interesting about our contextual inquiries was that during our interviews, all of our users were struggling to navigate through the site.

After collecting our findings, we decided to use affinity mapping to synthesize our research in order to pinpoint any patterns of frustration our users were facing.

#Competitive&Comparative


Throughout the research process, I discovered that Zara does not want to be compared to brands such as H&M, but rather wants to be seen as a higher-end, pseudo-luxury brand. We looked at how other brands were doing to address the three main pain points discovered in our research by doing a competitive and comparative analysis.

Next Steps


This case study proposed small, but impactful changes to provide a much better experience for users while keeping the brand image intact. Now with these proposed updates, users will have a straight forward shopping experience without making any large sweeping changes. This transformation will generate more revenue for Zara and turn browsers into buyers.

Some next steps for this project would be to explore Zara’s Join Life sustainability program as a prominent part of the shopping experience, as well as how Zara could accommodate more user demographics (i.e. bargain-hunter users).




Reflection and Learning


1) Small, but Powerful

I have been doing lots of usability testing and I learned that sometimes even the smallest change can have a meaningful impact for users.


2) Users and Business are Important

When it comes to designing, it is important to understand needs from both sides. For this project, our group wanted to make too many changes that would make Zara's website no longer look like what is it now. However, after doing competitive and comparative research, we got to learn more about the business needs and the importance of keeping the brand image intact. When it comes to redesigning, we have to carefully and be aware of the business needs.
